Annual Prepayment Option for Closed Mortgages:

Increasing your regular Payment Amount:
You may, once in every 12-month period starting on your Interest Adjustment Date or the anniversary of that date, increase
your regular payment by an amount up to 10% of the principal and interest portion of your monthly payment amount. If you
renew your mortgage, the renewal date will become your interest adjustment date.
Change your Payment Frequency:
You may change your payment frequency from monthly to weekly, bi-weekly, semi-monthly, accelerated bi-weekly or
accelerated weekly payments. The more frequent your payments, the faster you pay off your mortgage. If you currently have
weekly or bi-weekly payments, you may change to accelerated weekly or accelerated bi-weekly payments.
*Double-Up® Mortgage Payments:
You may prepay any amount between $100 and the equivalent of the principal and interest portion of your regular monthly
mortgage payments on any or every payment date. The extra payment is applied directly against the principal balance of
your mortgage.

A fixed rate mortgage offers security.
A fixed rate mortgage is a smart choice if you want security
in your mortgage. It offers you the benefit of locking in your
interest rate for the term of your mortgage. You don't have
to worry about interest rate volatility during your term, since
your interest rate stays the same.
Your payment amount stays the same and you'll know
exactly how much principal and interest you will be paying
on each payment date throughout the term you select.
A fixed rate mortgage guarantees that your amortization
will not change during the term of your mortgage - which is
great if your goal is to be mortgage free by a certain date.
The down side of a fixed rate mortgage is that you can't
take advantage of a drop in interest rates during your
existing term. Should rates decrease, with a variable rate
mortgage more of your payment would go to your principal,
which could result in a shorter amortization period and
reduce interest costs.
If you can't decide between a fixed or variable rate for your
next mortgage term, talk to us. We'll advise you on the
features and benefits of both fixed and variable rate terms
and help you decide which mortgage option best fits your
situation and risk tolerance.

Glossary of Terms
Conventional Mortgage - A mortgage with a down
payment of 20% or more of the value of the property.
Conventional Ratio / NHA-insured - A mortgage where
the down payment is less than 20% of the value of the
property.
Principal Paid - The total amount applied towards your
outstanding Principal Balance during the statement
period. This amount includes transactions such as
(but not limited to) Double Ups, Lump Sum Payments,
and Principal Payments.
Increase to Principal (new funds you requested) -
The total amount your principal balance has increased
as a result of any additional funds advanced to or
requested by you during the statement period.
Principals Payments - The total amount applied
towards your outstanding Principal Balance during the
statement period from your regular Principal & Interest
payments.
Prepayments - Means repaying part of the principal
amount ahead of schedule. Depending on the type of
mortgage you have, and the amount you are paying,
you may have to pay a prepayment charge when you
make a prepayment.
Skip-a-Payment - The number of times you requested
to skip a payment and the amount of interest added to
the principal you owe as a result.
Principal Balance Increases (due to reduced
payments)-Amounts added to the principal balance
you owe when your regular payment is changed. This
can happen if you lower your regular payment amount,
if the interest rate increases and your payment
doesn't, or you extend the period over which you're
paying off your mortgage.
Other Adjustments - Interest added to the principal
you owe because:
i) Your mortgage term ends on a date that is different
from your regular payment date.
ii) You switched from a fixed interest rate to a variable
rate (or vice versa) and the effective date is different
from your payment date.
Borrower's Charges - Any payments relating to your
property that you owe to someone else and that RBC
Royal Bank paid on your behalf. For example: condo
fees, late property taxes, repairs, maintenance fees
such as lawn care etc.
Interest Due, but Unpaid (Interest in Arrears) - Interest
that was due on your last payment date, but not paid
with your last payment.
Interest to be Paid (Accrued Interest) - The amount of
interest that has accumulated from the date of your
last regular payment to the end of this statement
period. This interest is to be paid with your next
regular payment.
Amortization Remaining - How long it will take to pay
off your mortgage, based on your current payment
amount, interest rate and payment frequency.
